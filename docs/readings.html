Readings

Required Textbook

GIS Fundamentals: A First Text on Geographic Information Systems (7th Edition)
Paul Bolstad & Steve Manson
Publisher: Baker & Taylor
ISBN: 978-1-59399-552-2
gisfundamentals.org

The textbook provides background on the concepts covered in lectures. It is a helpful reference for future GIS work, but is not strictly necessary to complete the lab exercises.

Recommended Textbook

Mastering ArcGIS Pro (2nd Edition)
Maribeth Price
Publisher: McGraw-Hill Higher Education
ISBN: 9781260587333

A practical reference for ArcGIS Pro procedures. Selected reading materials from this book will be provided by the instructor.

Readings by Day

Day 1 — Introduction to GIS

Day 1 Bolstad & Manson, Ch. 1: An Introduction to GIS — Overview of GIS concepts, components, and applications.

Day 1 Dent, Cartography: Thematic Map Design, Ch. 1: Introduction to Thematic Mapping — Foundations of map design and the role of thematic maps in communication.

Day 1 Monmonier, How to Lie with Maps, Ch. 1 & 3 — How maps can mislead, and the relationship between scale and generalization.

Day 2 — Projections & Data Entry

Day 2 Bolstad & Manson, Ch. 3: Geodesy, Projections, and Coordinate Systems — The shape of the Earth, map projections, and coordinate reference systems.

Day 2 Monmonier, How to Lie with Maps, Ch. 2: Projections — How projection choice affects map appearance and interpretation.

Day 3 — Data Management & Spatial Analysis

Day 3 Bolstad & Manson, Ch. 7: Digital Data — Sources and types of digital geographic data.

Day 3 Bolstad & Manson, Ch. 9: Vector Spatial Analysis — Buffering, overlay, and other vector operations.

Day 3 Dent, Cartography, Ch. 12 — Dot and proportional symbol mapping.

Day 3 Openshaw (1984), “Ecological Fallacies and the Analysis of Areal Census Data” — The risks of drawing individual-level conclusions from aggregate spatial data.

Day 4 — Network Analysis, Geocoding & Raster

Day 4 Bolstad & Manson, Ch. 10: Raster Analysis — Raster data models, reclassification, map algebra, and zonal operations.

Day 4 Ribeiro et al., “Tools for Address Georeferencing in Public Health” (PLoS ONE) — Review of geocoding tools and accuracy considerations for health research.

Day 4 Network Analysis tutorial (provided) — Step-by-step guide to ArcGIS Pro Network Analyst.

Day 5 — Spatial Estimation & Map Design

Day 5 Bolstad & Manson, Ch. 12: Spatial Estimation — Interpolation methods including IDW, splines, and kriging.

Day 5 Brewer, Designing Better Maps, Ch. 4: Color Basics — Principles of color use in cartography.

Day 5 Monmonier, How to Lie with Maps, Ch. 10: Data Maps — How classification and symbolization choices affect map interpretation.

Day 5 Monmonier, How to Lie with Maps, Ch. 11: Color — The role of color in map communication and potential misuse.

Day 5 Gelman & Price (1999), “All Maps of Parameter Estimates Are Misleading” (Statistics in Medicine) — Why visualizing uncertainty matters when mapping estimated parameters.

Additional References

These readings provide deeper background on topics covered in the course. They are optional but recommended for students planning to use GIS in their research.

  • Voss et al. (2006), “Census Exploratory Spatial Data Analysis”
  • Coombes et al. (1979), “Daily Urban Systems in Britain: From Theory to Practice”

Cartography and Visualization:
